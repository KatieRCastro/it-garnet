Planning a Visit

Get ready to embark on an unforgettable journey as you explore the breathtaking landscapes and exhilarating waters at Buffalo River. Whether you're a seasoned traveler or a first-time adventurer, our destination promises an experience like no other.

River Trails

Lace up your hiking boots and explore the numerous trails that meander through the river valley, leading you to hidden waterfalls, secluded swimming holes, and panoramic overlooks. For those who prefer biking, the river boasts an extensive network of trails perfect for both mountain biking and leisurely rides. Whether you're seeking a thrilling downhill descent or a scenic pedal along the riverbank, you'll find plenty of options to suit your skill level and interests.

Canoeing/Kayaking

In addition to hiking and biking, the river offers fantastic opportunities for canoeing and kayaking enthusiasts. The varying currents of the river provide the perfect setting for a relaxing or exhilarating paddle, allowing you to explore the beauty of the valley from a different perspective.

Ziplining

For those seeking an extra dose of adrenaline amidst the serene beauty of the river valley, ziplining offers an exhilarating way to experience the landscape from a whole new perspective. Imagine soaring through the air, feeling the wind rush past you as you take in panoramic views of the river below.

Visiting Trails

The park encompasses over 95,000 acres that surround the free-flowing Buffalo, much of which is linked together by a growing network of trails to accommodate hiking and equestrian-based recreational activities. These trail systems also offer numerous side hikes to locations that showcase the natural and human history that have shaped the region.

The only trails that allow dogs are the three Buffalo Point Campground trails (Forest Trail, Campground Trail, and Overlook Trail), the Tyler Bend Campground trails (Spring Hollow Trail, Buck Ridge Trail, and Rock Wall Trail), Mill Creek Trail, Villines Homestead Trail, and the Buffalo River Trail between Ponca and Steel Creek only.

Backcountry camping is allowed on the Buffalo River Trail and some of the park's other trails. Backcountry camping is not permitted on the Lost Valley Trail, Tyler Bend Campground Trails, Indian Rockhouse Trail, or Buffalo Point Campground Trails. Mobility devices are permitted in all areas.

Don't forget the sunscreen, bug spray, water, snacks, and sturdy footwear. We hope you have an enjoyable hike!

What is a Zipline Canopy Tour?

Our canopy tour is a system of cables and platforms, wherein the participant “glides” or “zips” through the treetops while connected to the cable via a harness. A large wooden platform serves as a landing base in each tree that you “zip to.”

How Long Does It Take?

The tour takes approximately 2 hours to complete, give or take a few minutes. Precisely how long it lasts is determined by a variety of factors, including weather and the physical ability of each participant.

The tour is for guests ages 7+ and who weigh between 70 and 250 lbs. We zip rain or shine and during rainy day zips the weight limit may be reduced to 200 lbs.

About the River

The Buffalo River was designated as America's first national river in 1972. It is managed by the National Park Service as the Buffalo National River. The river and its surrounding ecosystem support a diverse array of plant and animal life, including numerous species of fish, birds, and mammals. It is also home to a variety of unique geological features, such as caves and springs. Conservation efforts are ongoing to protect the ecological integrity of the Buffalo River and its surrounding watershed. The National Park Service, along with various conservation organizations and local stakeholders, work to preserve the river's natural resources and recreational opportunities for future generations. Overall, the Buffalo River holds significant cultural, recreational, and ecological importance, drawing visitors from across the country to experience its pristine beauty and outdoor activities.
